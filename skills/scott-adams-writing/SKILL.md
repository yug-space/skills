# Scott Adams Writing

Good writing is clear and persuasive, and the whole job is keeping it simple. A good argument in five sentences sways more people than a brilliant one in a hundred. Simple writing is persuasive writing. Don't fight it.

This skill applies Scott Adams' writing method (from "The Day You Became A Better Writer" and his persuasion work). Use it to edit or draft any prose meant to inform or persuade.

## The core rules

1. **Cut extra words.** Don't write "He was very happy" when "He was happy" says it. Kill "very," "really," "just," "actually," "quite," "in order to," "the fact that," "I think that." If a word can go without changing the meaning, it goes. Prune every sentence.

2. **One thought per sentence.** Write short sentences. Don't pack two ideas into one — split them. Readers are lazier and less attentive than you think; make them work less.

3. **Grab with the first sentence.** The opening's only job is to make the reader curious enough to read the next line. Rewrite it ten times if you must. Lead with the hook, never the throat-clearing.

4. **Object before action.** "The boy hit the ball" beats "the ball was hit by the boy." Brains picture the actor first, then the act. Prefer active voice and subject-verb-object order.

5. **Pick the vivid word.** Same idea, stronger word. For humor or punch, say "swill," not "drink." Concrete beats abstract. (Use vividness for effect, not decoration.)

## Persuasion layer (when the goal is to convince)

- **Simple is persuasive.** Fewer words and shorter sentences don't just read easier — they win arguments.
- **Lead with contrast, not just facts.** People are moved more by "X, not Y" comparisons than by raw data.
- **Repetition sticks.** Restate the key idea; don't assume one mention lands.
- **Write with confidence.** Hedged, qualified prose persuades no one. State it plainly.

## How to apply

When asked to improve text:

1. Read the draft's **first sentence** first. If it doesn't make you curious, rewrite it before touching anything else.
2. Return the **tightened version first**, then a short bullet list of what you cut/changed and why.
3. Convert passive voice to active unless the doer is genuinely unknown or irrelevant — flag each one.
4. Run the **checklist** below over the result.
5. Match and simplify the author's voice; don't replace it. Editing means removing, not decorating.

## Editing checklist

- [ ] First sentence makes the reader curious
- [ ] No sentence carries two thoughts
- [ ] Every "very / really / just / actually" deleted unless load-bearing
- [ ] Active voice; actor before action
- [ ] Strongest concrete word chosen over the abstract one
- [ ] Nothing left that can be cut without losing meaning
