# Scott Adams' Writing Method — Reference

Supporting material for the `good` skill. Source: Scott Adams, "The Day You Became A Better Writer" (Dilbert Blog), plus his persuasion writing (*Win Bigly*, persuasion-tips collections).

## The essay, distilled

> Business writing is about clarity and persuasion. The main technique is keeping things simple. Simple writing is persuasive. A good argument in five sentences will sway more people than a brilliant argument in a hundred sentences. Don't fight it.

Adams says this method covers ~80% of the rules of good writing. He learned it in a one-day business-writing course.

## The five rules, with examples

### 1. Get rid of extra words
Simple means removing words that add nothing.

- ❌ "He was very happy."
- ✅ "He was happy."

"very" feels like it adds something. It doesn't. Prune your sentences. Other common deletions: *really, just, actually, quite, basically, in order to, the fact that, there is/are, I think that.*

### 2. Write short sentences
Avoid putting multiple thoughts in one sentence. Readers aren't as smart — or as patient — as you'd think. One idea per sentence.

### 3. The first sentence must grab the reader
Its only job is to make the reader curious enough to read the second sentence. Adams rewrote the opening of the essay a dozen times. The hook beats the windup.

### 4. Learn how brains organize ideas (object before action)
Readers comprehend "the boy hit the ball" faster than "the ball was hit by the boy." It's easier to picture the object (the boy) before the action (the hitting). All brains work that way — so prefer active voice and subject-verb-object order.

Adams' own sly example: he wrote "All brains work that way," not "That is the way all brains work."

### 5. Word choice (especially for humor)
Humor writing is a lot like business writing — it needs to be simple. The difference is word choice. For humor, don't say "drink" when you can say "swill." Pick the concrete, vivid word.

## Persuasion principles (Scott Adams)

These extend the writing rules when the goal is to convince, not just inform:

- **Simple is persuasive.** Clarity and brevity move people. Complexity loses them.
- **People are persuaded by contrast, not facts.** Frame choices as comparisons ("this, not that").
- **Repetition is persuasive.** Say the key thing more than once.
- **Visual beats verbal.** A picture or vivid image out-persuades abstract argument.
- **Confidence persuades.** You have to appear to believe it for others to believe it. Cut the hedging.
- **Strategic ambiguity.** Leaving room for readers to fill in meaning can be more persuasive than spelling everything out — people supply their own best case.

## Quick self-edit pass

1. Does sentence 1 make me curious? If not, rewrite it.
2. Read each sentence — does it hold two thoughts? Split it.
3. Delete every weak intensifier and filler phrase.
4. Flip passive to active; put the actor first.
5. Swap abstract words for concrete, vivid ones.
6. Final sweep: cut anything that can go without losing meaning.
